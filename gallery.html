Gallery

1897

Wilbur Wright at work in the Wright Cycle Company, making bicycles. Note the bicycle frame overhead.

1898

The Wright home at 7 Hawthorne Street in Dayton, Ohio. Both the home and the bicycle shop were later moved to Greenfield Village at the Henry Ford Museum in Dearborn, Michigan.

1900

The Lifesavers at Kitty Hawk. These men, in fact, many of the people on the North Carolina Outerbanks, aided the Wright brothers during their visits.

1901

The Wrights moved their camp to the base of the Kill Devil Hills. Here Octave Chanute, a respected engineer and aeronautical experimenter, visited them for the first time.

1901

Dan Tate and Edward Huffaker launch Wilbur aboard the second glider. Tate was a "Banker" (a native of the Outerbanks) and Huffaker was a friend of Chanute's.

1901

Back in Dayton, the Wrights decided that the information they had been relying on to design their gliders was in error. They built this wind tunnel to test over two hundred wing shapes and generate their own design data.

1903

The Wrights initial sketch for their first powered airplane. It was drawn on brown wrapping paper.

1908

People came from all over Europe to watch Wilbur fly. He demonstrates the Flyer for thousands of people that include heads of state, royalty, and the commanders of armies.

1909

During the winter, Wilbur moves his flight operations to Pau in southern France. Later, he flies in Italy, eventually making over 100 flights in Europe.

1898

The Wright "Van Cleve" bicycle. The Wrights manufactured this model and one other, the "St. Clair," between 1896 and 1902.

1899

Wilbur designed this controllable kite to test the aerodynamic control system he would later use in gliders and airplanes.

1900

The first Wright glider being flown as a kite to measure its lifting capacity. The Wright found it would not support the weight of a man in a stiff wind.

1901

The second Wright glider, at the time that it was built, was the largest glider anyone had ever attempted to fly.

1901

Wilbur in flight aboard the second glider.

1901

The Wrights built several balances to use in the wind tunnel to measure various forces on the wing shapes. This was their "lift balance.".

1903

The Wright set up the Flyer and its launch track for the first flight attempt on December 14, 1903.

1909

Orville Wright, his leg and hip on the mend, joins Wilbur in Europe.

1909

The Wright return from Europe to a hero's welcome in Dayton, Ohio.

1909

The Wright Cycle Company at 1127 West Third Street in Dayton, Ohio. It was here that the Wrights built all their experimental gliders and airplanes.

1900

The Wrights travel to Kitty Hawk, North Carolina to test their first manned glider. This was their first camp, just outside of the village.

1900

The glider was all but destroyed when it tore loose from the ground in a storm. The Wrights repaired it an continued to make test flights.

1901

The Wrights fly their second glider as a kite to measure its lift. Again, they were disappointed.

1901

Wilbur just after he landed. The performance of the second glider was as disappointing as the first, and the Wright left Kitty Hawk sooner than they had planned.

1902

Wilbur and Dan Tate launch Orville aboard the third glider. The tail is now a single-panel rudder. The Wrights made it movable to improve the controls. It is now has a three-axis aerodynamic control system, making it the first fully controllable flying machine ever.

1903

When the Wrights couldn't find a suitable engine for their airplane, they built their own with the help of machinist Charley Tailor. The 200-pound, 4-cylinder engine produced 12 horsepower.

1905

The Wrights consider everything they've learned in the past seven year and build their third powered airplane -- the Wright Flyer III. It flies well and is much easier to control than their previous Flyers. This will be their last experimental airplane.

1909

Katharine Wright accompanies her convalescing brother Orville and take over as the Wright social director in Europe. Wilbur takes her aloft, making her one of the first women to fly.
